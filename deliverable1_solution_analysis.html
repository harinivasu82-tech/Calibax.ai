01 Problem inference — what we heard
"I run a $2.4 billion business and I still can't get a straight answer to a simple question without waiting days."
James Whitfield · CEO, FreshChoice Group · SDR Briefing Memo

On the surface, this reads as a technology complaint. But the briefing reveals something deeper: a structural intelligence gap — a disconnect between the pace at which FreshChoice generates events and the pace at which insight reaches the people who need to act. James Whitfield does not want a faster dashboard. He has had dashboards — the Tableau rollout 18 months ago was built for this, and it failed. What he described is categorically different: a conversation with his business. Unscripted. Immediate. Numbers with the human context that explains them.

Speed gap
1–2 business days to answer a question that should take minutes. The CEO must pre-schedule his curiosity. By the time the answer arrives, the decision window has often closed.
Silo gap
Finance in SAP. Suppliers in Salesforce. Transactions in POS. Supply chain in a legacy WMS. Operational reality in Teams, WhatsApp, and email. The human intermediary is the only integration layer.
Trust gap
The Tableau failure created scar tissue. Source attribution is not a UX feature — it is the prerequisite for rebuilding trust in any answer the system provides. Every number must be citable.
Reach gap
The bottleneck does not stop at the CEO. Every regional manager, store manager, logistics lead, and analyst faces a version of this problem calibrated to their role. It is a company-wide failure.
Our inference: FreshChoice does not have a reporting problem. It has an intelligence distribution problem. Data exists — 1.5 million transactions per day. The failure is in the last mile: translating raw data into timely, contextual, role-appropriate insight and getting it to the right person at the right moment.
02 Root cause — why this cannot be solved internally

Previous attempts — including Tableau — failed because they attacked the symptom (slow reports) without addressing the cause. Three structural blockers prevent FreshChoice from solving this alone:

1. The data team is mismatched to the task. Fifteen BI analysts skilled at producing scheduled reports — not data engineers or AI architects. They are built for pull-based reporting. The CEO wants push-based intelligence. That gap is not closed by a training programme.

2. The stack was never designed for unified access. SAP, Salesforce, the POS, and the WMS were each procured for a specific operational job. Unifying them through traditional ETL takes 18–24 months and significant capital. That is not the answer being sought here.

3. Unstructured data has never entered the intelligence picture. The context that explains why numbers move — a supplier delay in Teams, a staffing crisis in WhatsApp, a regional director's escalation email — lives entirely outside every data system. No BI tool can surface it. This is what makes the CEO's ask genuinely novel: he wants the number and the story behind it.

The opening: The convergence of LLMs, retrieval-augmented generation, and low-friction API connectivity has — for the first time — made it technically feasible to bridge all three gaps simultaneously, without requiring FreshChoice to replace its existing stack.

05 The full platform vision — Nexus

Nexus is Calibrax's enterprise intelligence platform — a reusable deployment across any large organisation with fragmented data and a human relay bottleneck. FreshChoice is the first deployment. The architecture is designed for configurability, not customisation.

Layer 1
Role-aware
personas
Every user sees a version calibrated to their role — CEO sees cross-market P&L and strategic signals; store manager sees their store only; driver sees their route. Same engine. Radically different surfaces. Access control enforced at the data row and column level.
Layer 2
Delivery
channels
Intelligence reaches users through whichever channel they already use: web app for the CEO, Teams bot for regional managers, mobile for store managers, WhatsApp for frontline staff, email digest for finance. The intelligence core is channel-agnostic.
Layer 3
Intelligence
core
Three engines in concert: a query engine (NL-to-SQL over structured data), a synthesis engine (RAG over email, Teams, documents), and an alert engine (proactive signals pushed before users ask). All three operate under a role-scoped access control layer.
Layer 4
Universal
connectors
Pre-built connectors for SAP, Salesforce, Teams, Outlook, WhatsApp Business, and common WMS/POS platforms. REST/webhook gateway for custom integrations. File ingest for CSV, Excel, PDF. No client ever migrates their data. Nexus reads where it lives.
Layer 5
Client data
sources
The client's existing systems, untouched. For FreshChoice: SAP (finance), Salesforce (supplier CRM), POS (transactions), WMS (supply chain), Teams + Outlook (comms), WhatsApp (store ops).

The full platform across all six personas, all data sources, and all delivery channels is a 12–18 month engagement. But — and this is the core of our commercial strategy — none of that needs to be built before the first cheque is written.

06 The prototype — this specific slice, and why

Of the entire Nexus platform, we have selected one precisely calibrated slice to build for the pitch. Not the easiest slice. The slice that, in 20 minutes with James Whitfield, produces: "this is exactly what I have been asking for."

The 14-day prototype
Nexus Command Console — CEO Intelligence Interface
A conversational web interface where the CEO types a natural language question and receives — within seconds — a grounded answer blending structured financial data with qualitative context from organisational communications. Every claim is source-attributed.
Built on: pre-computed structured data lookup (40–50 query variants) + RAG pipeline over a simulated Teams/email corpus + keyword-based query classifier + LLM synthesis layer + numeric hallucination guardrail.
01
It mirrors the CEO's exact words, not a paraphrase
Whitfield said: "I want to ask 'How did our fresh produce margins perform in NZ?' and get an actual number, with context. I want to ask 'What are the top three issues my regional managers are escalating?' and have it pull from their emails and Teams." The prototype does both, together. It is a direct response to his brief — not an approximation.
02
It addresses the trust deficit with source attribution
The CEO failed with Tableau because he could not trust the numbers. The source panel — showing exactly which file or message thread each part of the answer came from — is the mechanism that makes this demo categorically different from everything he has seen before.
03
It demonstrates the hardest part of the full platform first
Blended retrieval — structured + unstructured in one response — is the technically novel and commercially differentiating capability. If we show it working cleanly, everything else on the roadmap becomes a credible next step rather than a theoretical promise.
04
It is the trojan horse for a 38,000-person deployment
When Whitfield asks "can my regional managers use this too?" — the answer is yes, and the next persona's view is already mapped. The CEO-only prototype is not the end state. It is the proof that unlocks the full commercial conversation.
05
It is achievable at demo quality in 14 days
One data scientist, one engineer. Pre-computed lookups save 3 days versus a full NL-to-SQL engine. RAG pipeline over synthetic data. Keyword classifier. Clean React chat interface. This is scoped, challenged, and negotiated to a plan with zero wishful thinking.
