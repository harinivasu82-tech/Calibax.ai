T-5 minutes. My room read before James arrives. Four questions I answer before I say a word.

My question What I look for How I adapt
Who else is in the room? Is Sarah Chen (CSO) present? Any unexpected faces — IT, legal, finance? If Sarah is there, I acknowledge her — she is my internal champion. Unknown faces: I ask for quick intros before I start.
What is James's energy? Rushed, curious, sceptical, or warm? Rushed → I strip my setup to 45 seconds and open the product immediately. Curious → I let him explore. Sceptical → I don't oversell the intro.
How technical is the room? Is IT or the data team present alongside James? Technical audience → I can briefly reference RAG and the data architecture. Non-technical → outcomes only. No acronyms.
What did he just come from? Did Calibrax present slides first? Has James been in back-to-back meetings for hours? If he's already been briefed for an hour, I open the product within 60 seconds of sitting down. No extended setup. I get out of the way and let the product go to work.
Why I set up this way

The biggest demo killer is a PM who talks for 10 minutes before showing anything. James came up through finance and operations — he is commercially impatient and has sat through too many conceptual AI pitches that never showed him a working product. The moment I open the laptop, I am already differentiated. My job in the setup is to give him just enough context to feel the contrast, then get out of the way.

02 The five scenes I run
Scene 01 The Setup  — no product yet
90 sec max Goal: establish the pain
I establish the pain so viscerally that when the product appears, it lands as a relief — not a feature demo.
What I say
// I am standing. Laptop is closed. Direct eye contact with James.

"Before I show you anything — I want to read you something back."

"In your briefing with our team, you said: 'I run a $2.4 billion business and I still can't get a straight answer to a simple question without waiting days.'"

"That's not a technology problem. That's a structural intelligence gap — and it compounds across every decision you make, every day."

"What I'm going to show you in the next 20 minutes is not a dashboard. It's not a reporting tool. It's not another Tableau."

"It's what your business looks like when you can just... ask."

— I pause. I open the laptop. I turn it toward James. —
What I am managing
I name Tableau deliberately. James views that project as a failure. Naming it signals I have done my homework and I am not repeating that mistake. If he reacts, I have already pre-empted Branch 3.
I quote him back at himself. His own words create instant recognition — he feels heard before I have shown him a single feature. This is not flattery. It is proof of preparation.
I hold to 90 seconds, hard. Any longer and I am burning the goodwill I just built. The product is the pitch. I get to it.
Screen state at this moment
Idle state — Command Console loaded, empty chat area Suggested question chips visible. Alert strip active: NZ Margin Alert · Auckland DC Delay · SG E-Commerce · AU on track. Input field focused.
Note
The alert strip is already surfacing intelligence before James has typed a word. I do not narrate this — I let him notice it himself. That first moment of unprompted discovery is more powerful than anything I could say about it.
Scene 02 The First Question  — structured data
2–3 min Goal: prove it works
I give James a clean, fast, accurate answer to a financial question he has waited 2 days for in the past. Proof of concept. Trust begins here.
What I say
// I gesture toward the screen — or let James lean in naturally.

"You mentioned you've been frustrated by NZ fresh produce margins. Let's start there."

— I click the chip: "How did NZ fresh produce margins perform last month?" — // As the answer streams in, I say nothing. I let it land. This silence is the demo. — I wait for the response to complete —

"18.4% in March. Down 3.2 percentage points from Q3. The steepest single-month decline in that category in 12 months."

"That answer — with that context — would have taken your finance team the better part of a day to produce. It just took four seconds."

— I click to expand the source attribution panel —

"And this matters. Every number you see has a source. You can see exactly where it came from — which dataset, which period, which extraction. This system doesn't hallucinate. If it doesn't have the data, it tells you."

What I am managing
I let the streaming silence do the work. The 4-second response is the demo. I do not fill it with words. James will watch the tokens appear and that visual does more than any explanation I could offer.
I open the source panel deliberately. This is trust-building, not feature-showing. James is sceptical — his finance team will immediately ask "how do we know the numbers are right?" I defuse that objection before it becomes a blocker.
I do not explain the technology. He does not need to know it is RAG + structured lookup. He needs to feel that the answer is grounded and trustworthy. Those are different things.

Annotation
Data cards show numbers, not charts — fast to read, impossible to misread across a conference table.
Annotation
Source panel is open and I have pointed to it — every claim attributed, every number auditable. This is what separates this from a hallucinating chatbot.
Scene 03 The Wow Moment  — cross-signal synthesis
3–4 min Goal: convert the deal
I show what no other product can do — financial data and operational context joined in a single answer. This is the scene that closes the deal. I have designed the entire demo to arrive here.
What I say
// After Scene 2 lands — I lean forward slightly. This is my pivot.

"Now. That was a financial answer. Good, fast, cited. But here's what's actually different about this."

"That margin number doesn't exist in isolation. Your teams have been talking about what caused it — emailing, messaging in Teams, escalating up the chain. That context lives somewhere too. It's just been invisible."

"Let me show you what happens when I connect the two."

— I click: "Are the NZ margin issues connected to anything my regional team has flagged recently?" — // This response takes ~6 seconds. I stay silent. I watch James, not the screen. — I wait for the full response —

"The Northland supplier delay — flagged by Marcus Reid, our NZ Regional Ops Lead, on 10 March. A 6-day replenishment gap for leafy greens across 4 Auckland stores. $0.9 million in expedited freight costs, booked in March COGS. That accounts for approximately 75% of the margin variance."

"The escalation thread had 7 messages. The issue has been resolved as of last week. Partial margin recovery is expected in the first two weeks of April."

— I point to the expanded source panel: SAP / Teams / Email / WMS —

"Four sources. One answer. No analyst in the middle."

What I am managing
This is the emotional peak of the demo. James asked for exactly this in the SDR memo — almost verbatim. I am delivering his own words back to him as a live product. I watch his face, not the screen, during the 6-second response.
"No analyst in the middle" is my close. It is not a feature. It is the elimination of his current daily pain. I say it slowly, then I stop talking completely. I do not rush to fill the silence.
I watch for the lean. If James leans forward, nods, or starts reading the response himself — I slow down and let him sit in the moment. I do not rush to Scene 4 if he is still processing this one.

Key

Key

Scene 04 The Handoff  — James types his own question
2–3 min Goal: transfer ownership
James stops watching a demo and starts using a product. This is the moment it stops being my system and starts being his.
What I say
// I push the laptop gently toward James — or signal the engineer to turn it.

"I've been driving. Your turn."

"Ask it anything that's actually on your mind right now. Not a scripted question. Something you'd genuinely want answered tomorrow morning."

— I step back — I stay quiet — I let him type — // Whatever he asks, the system responds. I do not intervene, pre-empt, or hover. If the answer is imperfect, I go to Branch 1. — after his response returns —

"That's what this is. Not a demo environment you'd never use again. An interface you'd open every morning."

What I am managing
This is the highest-stakes, highest-reward moment. Nothing I say will be as convincing as James typing a question himself and getting a useful answer. My job here is to stay out of the way. The engineer stays invisible.
I do not hover. If he struggles to phrase the question, I do not help. I let him find his own words — that is part of the experience. Assisting him would undermine the point entirely.
If the answer is imperfect: I do not apologise. I say: "This is a 14-day prototype on synthetic data. With your live SAP and real Teams data, this gets significantly sharper. That's exactly what the discovery phase is for."
Screen state at this moment
Live response to James's own question Whatever he typed + the system's response. I do not script this screen. The product must stand on its own here.
Note
This is the trust moment. James is in control, I am not narrating. My red-team on Days 11–12 gave me confidence that the system handles off-script questions without embarrassing me. I trust that work now.
Scene 05 The Close  — vision, not features
2 min Goal: make the ask
I pull back from the prototype, paint Day 90, and make the ask. I do not let the demo be the finale — the vision is. The product is the proof. The close is the conversation.
What I say
// I take the laptop back. I stand. Direct eye contact.

"What you just used is a 14-day prototype built on synthetic data — a deliberate slice of what's possible."

"This isn't a better dashboard. It's an intelligence layer. Today it answers questions for you. In 90 days — with your live SAP data, your real Teams threads, your actual supply chain logs — it answers questions for every decision-maker in this business. Your regional managers. Your logistics leads. Your store teams. Same engine. Calibrated to each role."

"You told us you want a control tower for your business. That's what I'm building."

"The next step isn't a contract. It's a 4–6 week paid discovery — I connect to your live data, prove it on real numbers, and put a production-grade proof of concept in your hands. Low risk. High signal."

"I've shown you the what. The discovery is the how — with your data."

— I pause — direct close —

"James — are you in?"

What I am managing
"Are you in?" is intentional. Not "what do you think?" or "any questions?" — those invite deferral. "Are you in?" invites a decision. I use it exactly as written and I do not soften it.
Short. Declarative. One ask. I do not list features. I do not show a roadmap slide. I do not over-explain the commercial model. One question at the end, then I wait.
If Sarah Chen is in the room: I make brief eye contact with her when I say "every decision-maker in this business." She is the internal champion — acknowledging her in front of James reinforces her position when she takes this internally.
Screen state at this moment
Idle or previous response — it does not matter I am standing. I am not pointing at the screen. The laptop is closed or turned away.
Note
The demo is over. This is now a commercial conversation. I am not a PM demoing software — I am a deal captain asking for a decision.

04 Off-script branches I am ready for

Six scenarios. For each: the signal that tells me it is happening, exactly what I say, and why that response works.

Branch 1 The system gives a vague, hedged, or incomplete answer to a question
What I say
"This is a 14-day prototype on synthetic data. When I connect to your live SAP and real Teams threads in the discovery phase, this answer gets significantly sharper. But notice what it did do — it identified the right sources, it didn't make up a number, and it told you what it knew and what it didn't. That's the behaviour I've engineered for."
Why it works
I reframe the limitation as a feature — graceful failure and no hallucination are design choices, not bugs. I pivot directly to the paid engagement. I do not apologise. I do not look at the engineer.
Branch 2 "This is all fake data, right? When does it connect to our actual systems?"
What I say
"Yes — this runs on realistic synthetic data. The architecture is identical to what connects to SAP, Teams, and your WMS. The discovery is 4–6 weeks — I wire the real connectors, test against your live data, and deliver a production-grade proof of concept. What you're seeing today is the interface and the intelligence logic. The data plumbing is the next step."
Why it works
Anticipated objection, clean answer, direct path to commercial engagement. I am never defensive about synthetic data — it is an honest and deliberate choice, not a limitation I am embarrassed by.
Branch 3 "We tried something like this before. It didn't work." — the Tableau reference
What I say
"The Tableau project failed for two specific reasons — poor data integration and low adoption. What's different here: I don't ask your IT team to build the connectors, and I don't ask your users to change their workflow. They ask questions in plain English. No training, no dashboards, no maintenance burden. That project asked people to come to the data. This brings the data to them."
Why it works
I am not dismissing the failure — I am demonstrating that I understand exactly why it happened and why this is structurally different. This is the most credibility-building moment in the whole demo if it arises.
Branch 4 Sarah Chen starts probing on data architecture, security, or LLM provider
What I say
"Great question — I can go deep on the architecture in a technical deep-dive right after this. At this level: the data never leaves your environment, I'm not training on your data, and the LLM is the orchestration layer — not the data store. Happy to walk through the full technical spec in the session after this one."
Why it works
I preserve momentum. I do not get pulled into a technical rabbit hole during the 20-minute pitch window. I signal competence without derailing the commercial narrative. Brief, confident, clean defer.
Branch 5 "This is impressive. I'll need to loop in IT / Legal / Finance before deciding."
What I say
"Completely understood. Let me leave you with a one-page discovery scope summary — timeline, team, data access requirements, commercial terms. My ask is a decision by end of week, because the squad I've described is available to start in two weeks. If I miss that window, the timeline shifts by a month. I'd rather not lose that runway for you."
Why it works
I create genuine urgency without artificial pressure. Squad availability is real — I communicate it as a constraint, not a tactic. The one-page summary gives Sarah exactly what she needs to shepherd approval internally without me in the room.
Branch 6 "Can I try a few more questions? Can you show me [other feature]?"
What I say
"Absolutely. I built it to be explored." — then I step back and let him go.
Why it works
An extended demo is a buying signal — the strongest one I can get in the room. I do not cut it short to stay on script. This is the best possible outcome. I let it run for as long as James wants.
The 6–7 minute buffer in the timing plan exists precisely for this. If James is still exploring at minute 17, I let him finish his current question, then take the laptop back naturally: "That's exactly the kind of question I want you to be able to ask every morning. Let me take two minutes to tell you what the next step looks like." Scene 5 always happens — it takes 2 minutes and I will always find those 2 minutes. What I do not do is cut James off mid-exploration to deliver a prepared close. The buying signal is more valuable than the script.
05 My close protocol

Three steps. I execute all three before anyone stands up to leave the room.

1
I get a verbal commitment — before anyone leaves
I ask directly: "Are you in — at least in principle?"
If yes
"Excellent. I'll have the discovery scope to you and Sarah by end of tomorrow — timeline, team, data access, commercial terms. One page."
If maybe
"Understood. What's the one thing that would need to be true for this to be a clear yes?" — then I listen. I do not pitch again.
If no
"I appreciate your honesty. Can you tell me what would have needed to be different today?" — intelligence for the next pitch.
2
I hand my leave-behind to Sarah Chen — not James
Sarah is the internal champion who will shepherd this through FreshChoice's approval process after I leave the room. I give her:
  • The one-page discovery scope summary (see Section 06)
  • A printed copy of D1 — Solution Analysis for internal circulation
  • The demo link ?demo=true so she can replay it for stakeholders who were not in the room
3
I send a follow-up to James and Sarah — within 24 hours
Subject: FreshChoice × Calibrax — Discovery Scope

My email contains four things and nothing else:
  • One paragraph summarising what I demonstrated
  • The ask: 4–6 week paid discovery, CEO + Regional Manager personas, live SAP + Teams connection
  • Commercial terms — one line, exact figure from the Calibrax commercial team
  • A decision date: "I'd welcome a decision by [pitch day + 5 business days] to hold my squad's availability."
Why I designed my close this way

Most pitches die in the gap between a good demo and a signed engagement. The CEO is impressed, leaves the room, gets pulled into other priorities, and my follow-up email sits in his inbox for three weeks. My close is designed to eliminate that gap. A verbal commitment in the room, a leave-behind that Sarah can action independently, and a 24-hour follow-up with a hard decision date — these three steps compress the decision cycle from "three weeks of drift" to "a week of deliberate process." I communicate the squad availability constraint as a real constraint — not a pressure tactic — because it is one.
